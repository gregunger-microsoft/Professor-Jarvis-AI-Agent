Introduction
Mary Shelley’s Frankenstein is a novel that explores science, responsibility, and the consequences of human ambition. The story focuses on Victor Frankenstein, a scientist who becomes obsessed with creating life and fails to consider the outcomes of his actions. The novel suggests that scientific ambition can become dangerous when it is not balanced with moral responsibility. This report will examine this idea by briefly summarizing the plot and discussing the characters and major themes of the novel.

Plot Summary
Frankenstein tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a young scientist who is determined to discover the secret of life. After years of study, he succeeds in creating a living creature but is immediately repulsed by its appearance and abandons it. The creature must survive on its own and learns about human society by observing others and reading books. Despite wanting companionship, the creature is rejected by everyone it encounters. As a result, it becomes angry and seeks revenge on Victor by killing several of his loved ones. Victor attempts to stop the creature but ultimately fails, and both characters suffer tragic consequences by the end of the novel.

Character Analysis
Victor Frankenstein is an intelligent and ambitious character, but his ambition often overrides his sense of responsibility. He is determined to achieve scientific greatness, yet he avoids accountability once his experiment succeeds. The creature is commonly described as a monster, but it also displays human emotions such as loneliness and a desire for acceptance. Elizabeth Lavenza represents kindness and moral goodness, although she does not have a strong influence on the story’s events.

Themes and Symbols
One of the main themes in Frankenstein is the danger of unchecked ambition. Victor’s desire to create life leads to destruction and suffering for himself and others. Another important theme is isolation, which affects both Victor and the creature throughout the novel. The creature’s isolation eventually drives him to violence. Light and fire function as symbols of knowledge, showing that knowledge can be both beneficial and harmful.

Textual Evidence
Victor reflects on his actions when he states, “Learn from me, if not by my precepts, at least by my example” (Shelley). This quotation suggests that Victor understands the consequences of his ambition, even though he realizes this too late. The creature also explains his suffering when he claims that he was “benevolent and good” before being rejected by society (Shelley).

Critical Evaluation
The novel presents a warning about the responsibilities that come with scientific advancement. Although Victor’s experiment is fictional, the story raises concerns about pursuing knowledge without considering ethical consequences. Some readers may sympathize with Victor’s intentions, but the novel makes it clear that his refusal to care for his creation contributes greatly to the tragedy.

Personal Reflection
Reading Frankenstein encouraged me to think about modern scientific developments, including artificial intelligence and biotechnology. Scientists today face similar ethical questions about responsibility and long-term impact. The moment when Victor abandons the creature stood out to me because it clearly shows how ignoring responsibility can lead to serious consequences.

Conclusion
Frankenstein demonstrates that ambition without responsibility can result in harm and suffering. Through Victor Frankenstein and his creation, Mary Shelley warns readers about the dangers of uncontrolled scientific pursuit. The themes of the novel continue to be relevant as society continues to advance scientifically.
